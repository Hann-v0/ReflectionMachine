Results of Thesis¶

The Functional Preference of Policemen when Using a Reflection Machine in Preventive Frisking¶

This is a notebook containing the results of the thesis of Hannah Venhuizen studying Artificial Intelligence at the Radboud University. In this thesis, an experiment took place in which nine students and five policemen participated. It contains both the data of the output of an application used in this thesis and a survey filled in twice by each participant.

Research setup¶

The research setup is defined into two parts. In one part, the focus is on the TYPE of questions/notifications (from now on called part A). In the other part, the focus is on the FREQUENCY with which a questions/notifications is posed (from now on called part B). All participants participate in each part. However, these parts can be presented in two different orders:

  • type (A) -> frequency (B) or;
  • frequency (B) -> type (A)

After each part, a survey is filled out with questions about the experiment. Part A consists of three blocks and part B consists of two blocks. Every block contains 4 cases. If you want to know more details, I refer you to the written thesis.

For now, it is important to stress again that there are two different sets of data for every participant. One is of the application, the other of the surveys. Section 1. Survey is both about the student and the policemen data.

# a function which transforms the ratings to actual answers
def transform(a, indicator,participant=-1,student=False, labels=False, number=0):
    indicator+=1
    s = ""
    lala=["","","","","","","","","",""]
    if number==1:
        if student==True:
            lala=s_dss_labels
        else: lala=p_dss_labels
    if number==2:
        if student==True:
            lala=s_no_dss_labels
        else:
            lala=p_no_dss_labels
    if participant!=-1:
        s = "Participant " + str(participant) + " " 
    agree = ["Strongly disagree; ", "Disagree; ", "Neutral; ", "Agree; ", "Strongly agree; "]
    amount = ["The less the better; ", "Neutral; ", "The more the better; "]
    student_amount = ["The less the better; ", "I like less; ", "Neutral; ", "I like more; ", "The more the better; "]
    quality = ["Very bad; ", "Bad; ", "Neutral; ", "Good; ", "Very good; "]
    counter=0
    for i in a:
        s+=lala[counter]
        counter+=1
        i = int(round(i))
        if indicator<4 or indicator==5 or indicator>6:
            if i != -1:
                lab=agree
                s+=agree[i-1]
        elif indicator==4:
            if student:
                lab=student_amount
                s+=student_amount[i-1]           
            else:
                lab=amount
                s+=amount[i-1]
        elif indicator==6:
            lab=quality
            s+=quality[i-1]
            
    if labels==True:
        return lab
    return s

1.0 Sub-questions¶

Three sub-questions can be answered with use of the data from the surveys. The questions are:

  1. What type of reflective questions/notifications do policemen prefer?
  2. How often should the RM pose a reflective question/notification?
  3. Does the experience of interacting with a DSS influence the experience of the RM?

Half of the participants is (falsely) informed that a Decision Support System (DSS) is included, where the individuals in the videos are selected by an algorithm. The other half of the participants is (truly) informed that we selected the individuals randomly out of the videos, so without help of a DSS.

We questioned the participants in a survey after each part (A and B). The surveys include statements about the application, where the participants responded by giving a ranking. The statements are as follows (where some aspects of the questions depend on the DSS condition: e.g. "I think the selection made by the algorithm is very accurate"):

1.1 What type of reflective questions/notifications do participants prefer?¶

We will answer this sub-question for both students and policemen. In order to do that, we analyze statement 9: "You saw the following reflective questions / notifications. Please rank them in order of relevance."

This statement is only presented in the survey after the participant completed block 1. So every participant answered this statement exactly once. The questions which need to be ranked are:

  • C1 Are you sure about your decision (not) to frisk this person? Why (not)?
  • C2 How certain are you about your decision (not) to frisk this person? Why?
  • C3 Are you sure your focus will lead to an optimal decision? Why (not)?
  • P1 Consider that [X] % of all people of the population walking past this spot [...]
  • P2 Do you think this person deviates from the population in a particular aspect? Why (not)?
  • P3 Consider that [X] % of all weapons carriers of this population [...]
  • H1 Up to now, you have decided (not) to frisk [X] % of all the people presented to you
  • H2 Consider that [X] % of all the people you decided (not) to frisk up to now [...]

Where C, P, H mean Certainty based, Population based an History based respectively. The 1, 2, and 3 are the indicators of the instance of that type. Of course, the labels are not presented to the participant. Before we begin, we load the data:

def yesno(a):
    s=np.array([])

            if j>0:
                s+=" "
            s+=str(j) +" & "  
        b=np.append(b,s[0:len(s)-3])
    return b

1.3 Cluster motivations¶

In order to understand the decisions of the participants, we have to dive deeper into the motivations behind the decisions whether to frisk or not. After eye-balling the motivations of the possible answers participants gave, we divided it into 6 clusters why people chose to frisk an individual; and 5 distinct clusters why people chose not to frisk an individual. In some cases, participants gave multiple reasons why they wanted to (not) frisk a particular individual. The 6 clusters with reasons to frisk are:

  • 1) distinct pace
  • 2) individual is alone
  • 3) suspicious focus
  • 4) distinct use of -or attention towards- accessoires
  • 5) deviant behaviour
  • 6) other, e.g. information told upfront; information told during experiment; gut feeling; it will not hurt to do it; etc.

Whereas the clusters with reasons NOT to frisk are:

  • -1) not the type to be carrying a weapon
  • -2) factual description of the situation
  • -3) no deviant behaviour
  • -4) cannot find a specific reason to frisk
  • -5) individual seems to be goal-oriented

Because the motivation is optional, sometimes a motivation is not given, then we label it with:

  • 0) no reason is given

def motivation_of_cluster(df,i,p=-1):
    i=round(i)
    if i>0:
        j =" " + str(i)
        if p!=-1:
            return df.loc[df["Motivation " + str(p)].str.contains(j)]
    else:
        j =str(i)
        if p!=-1:
            return df.loc[df["Motivation " + str(p)].str.contains(j)]
    return df.loc[df["Motivation 0"].str.contains(j) | 
                   df["Motivation 1"].str.contains(j) |
                   df["Motivation 2"].str.contains(j) | 
                   df["Motivation 3"].str.contains(j) | 
                   df["Motivation 4"].str.contains(j)]
